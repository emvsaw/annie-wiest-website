
## When the Mountain Wants You Dead

#### Aiden

Aiden Raines trusted checklists and winter sky colors, but the sky outside Sacred Peaks Ranch was lying. It hung purple-gray and calm, feigning innocence while frost spiked the roofline like shrapnel. Aiden knew the difference between beauty and a warning. That sky promised to bury anyone who didn’t know better.

The great room smelled of pine and cinnamon. Stevie must have lit one of those scented candles to go with the actual boughs draped over the mantle. Comforting, if you didn’t know how fast a mountain could turn.

Aiden paused in the doorway, coffee in one hand, the other hooked in their back pocket. Casual was the trick. You could walk into a burning building as long as your shoulders were loose.

Twenty retreat guests milled under the exposed beams, their carefully arranged yoga cushions turning the rough-hewn space into a curated catalog spread.

Aiden moved through them, scanning. The threats jumped out at them: the smoke detector above the mantle blinking a low-battery warning; firewood stacked steeply enough to break a toe with one careless hip-check; slush tracking across the slate entrance, invisible against the dark stone until someone slipped.

On the wall-mounted screen, the forecast glowed in orange and black. _CAIC Warning. Considerable avalanche danger. High urgency._

Three hours ago, the wind had shifted. Now the ridge above them held enough unstable snow to erase a tour bus. These people drinking oat milk lattes had no idea they were sitting at the base of a loaded gun.

“They’re ready for you,” June appeared at their elbow.

Aiden hadn’t heard her approach. They turned, masking the startle with a quick sip. June had that look, the one from the night before their calculus finals, when she’d needed someone to tell her she’d be fine.

“They aren’t ready.” Aiden’s voice dropped low.

June followed their gaze toward a guy in a too-clean fleece now sidling dangerously close to the slush. “That’s why you’re here. Scare them straight.” She winced. “But gently? They’re paying a premium for wellness.”

“Physics isn’t gentle,” Aiden said. They set the mug down on the side table. “You want gentle, hire a poet.”

“Already did, you know.” June tipped her chin toward the second row of chairs. “She’s up after you.”

Aiden’s gaze followed.

The woman June meant didn’t look like a poet. She looked like she could hike Aiden into the ground. Field pants, boots with real tread, a charcoal sweater made for moving. Dark hair braided over one shoulder. She held a notebook, thumb rubbing the paper’s edge. Her eyes, warm brown and sharp, tracked the room: exits, weak points, the same scan Aiden had just done.

They swallowed hard. “That’s the archaeologist?”

“Kiona Suchil.” June’s smile softened. “State Historical Fund sent her, but don’t let the calm face fool you. She’ll fight you for her site if she has to.”

“Good.” Another sip to steady their pulse. “Someone else here who understands stakes.”

“Play nice,” June murmured and patted their flannel-covered bicep, then stepped toward the front of the room. “Okay, everyone, let’s get started.”

The chatter faded, leaving only the pop of the fire and the wind rattling the pines against the glass outside.

Twenty pairs of eyes turned. Some hopeful, most tired. The kind that wanted transformation without risk.

In the second row, the woman looked up from her notebook. Her pen stilled. That assessing gaze met Aiden’s and held.

Aiden’s next breath stuck halfway. Like the panic of a drop or the heat of a fire line. They were looking at trouble they couldn’t control, and they knew it the way they knew unstable snow, instantly, in the body, before the brain caught up.

“Welcome to Sacred Peaks,” June said, voice warm as the hearth. “Before we talk about yoga and equine therapy and Kemi’s incredible cooking, we need to talk about respect. For this land, and for the weather that shapes it.”

She nodded toward Aiden.

“This is Aiden Raines. Eighteen years wildland firefighting, search-and-rescue certified, and currently the only thing standing between you and a very expensive medevac flight. We want you back at dinner in one piece. So, when Aiden talks, you listen. When they tell you to stop, you stop. When they tell you to move...”

“You move,” Aiden finished. Their voice carried without effort, low and certain. They didn’t smile. Safety wasn’t a sales pitch.

Aiden stepped into the center of the room, the familiar hum of adrenaline that came before a drop.

“My name is Aiden,” they said, their deep voice carrying without a mic. “And right now, the mountain we’re standing on would be very happy to kill you.”

A nervous laugh rippled from the third row but died fast. Good.

“We’re under a moderate to high avalanche advisory.” Aiden clicked the remote. The screen behind them flared with topographic lines and red danger zones. “Three hours ago, the wind shifted. Step outside right now and you’ll smell it. Crisp, metallic, like snow on slate right before it slides.” They tapped the scarlet ridge. “That wind is stacking weight on the ridge. One wrong step and it all comes down.”

Silence. Better.

“Best case,” Aiden continued, “my crew digs you out before you run out of air. Worst case...” They let it hang. Let them imagine. “Your Instagram followers get a memorial post.”

Someone shifted uncomfortably. The expensive boots guy looked pale.

Aiden’s thumb traced the old scar along their knuckle. Ryan was now three years gone and his lighter in their pocket was cold metal against their palm. The ache never quite left. “Stay on marked paths. Keep your beacons on. And check the daily updates I’ll post by the beverage station. Every morning. Before you lace up.”

They stepped back. Protocol delivered. Liability reduced. Pulse still too fast.

“Thank you, Aiden.” June’s smile didn’t waver, though Aiden had definitely not been gentle. “Next, we have Dr. Kiona Suchil, consulting archaeologist from the State Historical Fund. Kiona’s here to ensure that as we enjoy this land, we don’t erase the history beneath our feet.”

Kiona Suchil stood. When she reached the front, she didn’t fidget. Didn’t smile for approval. Just clicked the remote.

A pottery shard and a bellied vase appeared on screen. Black-on-white. Geometric angles sharp as new.

“Good afternoon.” Her voice was low and warm. “When we talk about wellness and grounding, we use those words lightly. I’m here to tell you what you’re literally grounding into.”

She gestured to the shard. “This fragment is eight hundred years old. Mesa Verde. Part of a water jar that carried someone through a winter harder than this one.” She pointed to a sharp angle in the design. “The person who painted this line... They did it with intention. They were speaking to the future. To us.”

Aiden leaned against the cold stone of the hearth, arms crossed. Usually history lectures slid right off their brain. Dates. Dust. Things that didn’t matter when you were trying to keep people breathing.

But Kiona wasn’t lecturing. She was...

“We’ve identified an Ancestral Puebloan site on the north ridge,” Kiona said, her voice picking up speed, warming, barely controlled urgency bleeding through. “Twelfth century. The thermal engineering alone is problem-solving on a genius-level, preserved in stone and adobe. I completed preliminary documentation last week. The site’s significance exceeds what aerial photography suggested.”

Her hands moved as she spoke, tracing shapes in the air. Enthusiasm broke through her calm, then she smoothed it back down like a stray hair. Her calm seemed like armor.

“The site is fragile,” she went on. “Significant. Unprotected.” Click. A new image. Snow-covered ruins, barely visible against white. “Every step you take off-trail doesn’t just risk avalanche. It risks crushing a story that’s waited eight centuries to be heard.”

She paused. Looked around the room. Her gaze landed on Aiden for half a second, assessing and sharp, before moving on.

“We don’t treat these sites as ruins. We treat them as elders.” She dropped her volume. Each word distinct and firm. “You wouldn’t walk across your grandmother’s table with muddy boots. Don’t do it here.”

The silence weighed different. Heavier.

“Preservation isn’t passive,” Kiona said. “It’s an active choice to let the past exist alongside us.”

She clicked the remote off. Looked at June. “Thank you.”

Aiden’s next breath came shallow. Had come shallow since the words ‘thermal engineering.’

“Questions?” June asked.

A hand shot up. Bearded guy in a too-new fleece. “Can we visit the site? If we’re careful?”

Kiona’s expression held steady. Only her shoulders drew back. Guarded. “Not without a certified guide. The soil stability is compromised by freeze-thaw cycles.”

“What about safety?” Another guest, this one genuinely curious. “If the site’s on the ridge where the wind loading is...”

Kiona turned. Looked directly at Aiden.

It was a test. “Aiden? How do we balance access with avalanche risk?”

Every eye in the room swiveled.

Aiden straightened. “We don’t balance it,” they said, keeping their voice flat. “We prioritize your pulse. If the ridge is unstable, the site is closed. History doesn’t go anywhere.” They met Kiona’s eyes, held them. “You do.”

Kiona didn’t bristle at the hard line. Instead, she nodded, a small, approving tilt of her head. “Agreed. We protect the site by protecting the people who might damage it. Boundaries keep both safe.”

Aiden looked away first.

***

Aiden headed for the coffee station near the entrance but stopped short.

Kiona stood by the glass display case next to the carafe, leaning in close enough that her breath fogged up the glass. Inside sat the metate, the flat massive grinding stone June’s family on her mother’s side had kept for generations. Kiona’s hands were clasped behind her back, like she was physically restraining herself from touching the glass.

Aiden approached quietly, but Kiona spoke without turning. “They kept the context.” Her voice was almost reverent. “That’s the rare part. Usually these end up in a museum drawer in Denver. Keeping it here, letting it stay home. That matters.”

Aiden stepped up beside her, close enough to smell earth and woodsmoke. Sage. “Does it?”

Kiona turned. Up close, the intensity of her stare was startling. “Yes. Without the land, it’s just a rock. With the land, it’s a witness to survival.”

Aiden looked at the stone. Looked at Kiona’s hands. No rings, nails short and practical, a thin white scar across her left knuckles.

“Coffee,” Aiden said, nodding toward the pot.

“Please.”

They reached for the carafe at the same time, hands nearly colliding.

Aiden pulled back. “Yours.”

“Black?” Kiona asked, pouring.

“Only way that makes sense.”

Kiona’s smile was quick, real. “Good answer.”

Kiona poured a second cup.

She took a sip, eyes assessing Aiden over the rim. “You handle a room well. I expected more shouting. Most safety officers like the sound of their own voice.”

“Shouting means you’ve already lost control.” Aiden took a sip. Hot, bitter, grounding. “If I have to shout, something’s on fire or someone’s falling.”

“You don’t waste energy.” Kiona’s mouth curved slightly. “I like it.” She nodded toward the window where Aiden’s truck was visible in the lot. A battered, lifted rig that looked like it had survived several apocalypses. “That yours?”

“The Beast? Yeah.”

“Your truck is older than my PhD.”

Aiden smirked into their mug. “But has better mileage.”

“Debatable. My dissertation covered four hundred years of people moving across this land. That’s a lot of mileage.”

“Paper mileage, though. The Beast has actual mud on the tires.”

“Touché.” Kiona’s smile widened, opened her face into angles and warmth, dangerous and inviting.

For a second, Aiden forgot to check the perimeter. Forgot to scan the exits. They were just looking at the way firelight caught in Kiona’s dark eyes.

“You two play nice,” Stevie’s voice cut in.

Aiden blinked. Stevie approached with her clipboard, June drifting beside her with an arm around Stevie’s waist.

“We need to coordinate schedules.” Stevie tapped her clipboard. “Kiona, you need access to the north ridge for your documentation. Aiden, you need to clear the area for safety. The contractor wants to start work March first. You’re running out of time.”

“I can work with that.” Kiona straightened, voice dropping back to professional register. “I need about three hours up there for some last preliminary grid photos. They’re my priority.”

“You heard the forecast.” Aiden set their cup down, the distance settling back between them like cold air from the window. “Stevie, did you get the last update?”

Stevie ripped a page off her clipboard and handed it over. “CAIC upgraded the warning. Storm front accelerated. It’s coming in twenty-four hours, not forty-eight.”

Aiden scanned the report. Pressure drop. Wind speed increasing. The sky was still purple-slate. But Aiden read between the clouds and the numbers screamed danger.

“We can’t wait two days.” Aiden’s jaw tightened. “If we don’t get up there tomorrow, we might not get up there for a week. And if that cornice blows...”

“The site gets buried.” Kiona’s voice went tight. “And the contractor bulldozes it in March before I can finalize the winter shelter context.”

“Dawn, then.” Aiden met her eyes. “Before the daylight destabilizes the snowpack.”

“I can be ready.”

June’s gaze moved from Aiden to Kiona and back with a softness that made Aiden’s ears burn. “Sounds like you two are partners for the day.”

“Keep her safe, Aiden,” Stevie said. “That site’s right on the edge.”

“I know the terrain. I’ve flagged the perimeter.”

“And I know the history,” Kiona added. “I won’t take unnecessary risks.”

“Good.” June’s smile landed squarely on the space between them. “Early start, then.”

Both ranch owners moved off toward the kitchen where someone was having a crisis regarding food allergies. Silence settled, heavy with the new timeline. Twenty-four hours until the weather turned lethal.

Kiona set her empty mug on the tray. “Dawn, then.”

“0500,” Aiden corrected. “I’ll have the gear prepped. Meet me at the truck.”

“I can drive myself...”

“One vehicle. Smaller footprint, fewer variables.” Aiden’s voice left no room for argument. “Plus my radio has a booster. Yours relies on cell towers that don’t exist up there.”

Kiona hesitated, checking the logic. “Fine. Efficient.”

“Safe,” Aiden corrected.

“Same thing, usually.” Kiona pulled out her phone. “Put your number in. In case the world ends before five AM.”

Aiden took the phone. The screen was warm from her pocket. They punched in their digits, thumb moving too fast. “If the world ends, I’ll be the one calling you.”

“Comforting.” Kiona took the phone back. Their fingers brushed again. The static snapped between them, sharp enough to make Aiden’s fingers twitch. It lingered in the air like ozone before a lightning strike.

“I should pack.” Kiona looked at Aiden with intensity. “You’re not what I expected, Raines.”

“What did you expect?”

“Someone louder. Less...” She gestured vaguely.

“The mountain’s loud enough.”

Kiona smiled, softening her face, and Aiden forgot to exhale. “See you at dawn, partner.”

She headed for the stairs, that deliberate walk. Didn’t look back.

Aiden looked to the window. The sky had shifted from pewter to bruised charcoal. Wind rattled the pines against glass.

The old ache in their hand flared with the drop in barometric pressure.

Eighteen years, they’d been the one running into fire. The one focused on containment and control. But watching Kiona’s confident stride disappear up the stairs, Aiden had the feeling that for the first time in a long time, the usual protocols weren’t going to be enough.
